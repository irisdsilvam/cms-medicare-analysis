Key findings — spending anomaly
Specialty mismatch outlier: Prescriber-001 (Family Practice) recorded $41.7M — 27x the anomaly threshold. Family Practice prescribers at cost levels comparable to oncology specialists represent a high-priority flag for program oversight review.
Specialty concentration in top outliers: 7 of the top 10 highest-cost prescribers are Hematology-Oncology specialists, consistent with high-cost chemotherapy agents. Notable exceptions: a Nurse Practitioner at $13.9M (rank #4) and an Ophthalmology specialist at $12.8M (rank #7).
Anomaly prevalence: 510 prescribers (2.4% of 500K records) exceed the $1.54M threshold (mean + 2σ), representing a disproportionate concentration of Medicare Part D spending warranting further investigation aligned with fraud, waste, and abuse detection objectives.

Key findings — geographic risk
Southern states lead in anomaly rate: Alabama (5%), Kansas (4.5%), and Kentucky (4.1%) show the highest concentration of anomalous prescribers as a share of their total prescriber population — all significantly above the national average of 2.4%.
Volume vs. rate distinction: California and New York have the highest absolute counts (57 and 45 anomalous prescribers respectively) but lower rates (2.7% and 3.0%), reflecting their larger prescriber populations. Rate-based analysis corrects for this bias and surfaces smaller states with disproportionate risk.
Per-1,000 density confirms southern pattern: Kentucky ranks #1 in prescriber density (31 per 1,000), followed by Alabama (50) and Kansas (45). This normalization reinforces that geographic concentration of anomalous prescribing is highest in the South and Midwest — actionable intelligence for regional program oversight prioritization.
